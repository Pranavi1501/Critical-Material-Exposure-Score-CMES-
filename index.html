Model overview
How the CMES score is constructed

The Critical Material Exposure Score (CMES) is a composite index that quantifies a building's supply chain vulnerability to critical mineral disruption. It produces a single score between 0 and 100, where higher scores indicate greater risk. The methodology is modelled on the planetary boundaries framework — each dimension has a defined safe operating space, and the score measures how far the building's material specification has pushed past that boundary.

The calculation proceeds in three stages: first, material quantities are derived from technology-specific intensity factors; second, each material is scored across six risk dimensions; third, scores are aggregated into a single CMES value using dimension weights derived from policy and literature.

CMES = Σ (w_d × D_d)
where D_d = Σ (kg_m / kg_total) × dim_score(m, d)   for each material m
and w_d = dimension weight (see table below)

Safe operating space boundary: score = 60  ·  Maps to single-origin concentration ≥ 60% threshold
Stage 1
Material quantity derivation

Material quantities (kg) are calculated by multiplying technology-specific intensity factors against installed capacity. Intensity factors are sourced from NREL LCA Harmonisation studies and IEA technology reviews, representing global average compositions.

kg_m = Σ (intensity_m,t × capacity_t)
e.g. Silicon (kg) = 5.5 kg/kWp × 40 kWp = 220 kg
e.g. Cobalt (kg) = 0.068 kg/kWh × 100 kWh = 6.8 kg

Intensity factors carry uncertainty ranges of ±15–25% depending on technology vintage. Values used represent 2023–2024 global averages at IEA confidence level 3–5.

Stage 2
Six-dimension risk scoring

Each material is scored on six dimensions on a 0–100 scale. Scores are derived from underlying indicators normalised to a common scale, where 100 = maximum risk and 0 = no risk. A mass-weighted average across all materials present gives the building-level dimension score.

Boundary justification
Why 60 is the safe operating space threshold

The safe operating space boundary on the planetary boundaries chart is set at score 60. This is not arbitrary — it maps to the point at which a single country or entity controls 60% or more of either the mining or refining of a given material. This threshold is the point at which supply chain resilience frameworks — including the UK Critical Minerals Strategy and the EU CRMA — treat concentration as a critical procurement risk requiring diversification or stockpiling policy.

Below this threshold, supply disruption from any single geopolitical event is generally absorbable. Above it, the loss of that single supply node creates a market shock that affects all buyers simultaneously, regardless of their individual sourcing strategy. This is the same logic applied in the planetary boundaries framework: crossing a boundary does not guarantee disaster, but it increases the probability of non-linear, hard-to-reverse consequences.

Dimension scores below 60 indicate the building's material specification remains within tolerable operating bounds for that dimension. Scores above 60 — the segments of the wedge extending past the dashed ring — represent genuine procurement risk that a design decision made today could reduce.

Live calculation trace
Your building — step by step

The following shows the exact arithmetic used to produce your CMES score, using the values you entered. Every number is traceable to the source data shown in the Methodology tab.

Step 1 — Material quantities
What's in your building

Each technology's installed capacity multiplied by its material intensity factor (kg per unit) gives total material mass.

Step 2 — Mass weighting
Material fractions

Each material's share of total mass determines how much it pulls the dimension scores. High-mass materials dominate; trace materials have minimal effect.

Step 3 — Dimension aggregation
From materials to dimensions

For each of the six dimensions, a mass-weighted average of individual material scores gives the building-level dimension score. Higher-mass materials pull the dimension score harder.

Step 4 — Final score
CMES computation

The six dimension scores are combined using their policy-derived weights to produce the final CMES score.
