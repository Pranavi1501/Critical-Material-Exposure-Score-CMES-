Model overview
How the CMES score is constructed

The Critical Material Exposure Score (CMES) is a composite index that quantifies a building's supply chain vulnerability to critical mineral disruption. It is modelled on the planetary boundaries framework — each of six dimensions has a defined safe operating space, and the score measures how far the building's material specification has pushed past that boundary.

The calculation proceeds in three stages: material quantities are derived from technology-specific intensity factors; each material is scored across six risk dimensions; scores are aggregated using dimension weights derived from policy and literature.

CMES = Σ (w_d × D_d)
where D_d = Σ (kg_m / kg_total) × dim_score(m, d) for each material m
and w_d = dimension weight (see table below)

Safe operating space boundary: region-specific single-origin concentration threshold (see region tab)
Stage 1
Material quantity derivation

Material quantities (kg) = technology intensity factor × installed capacity. Intensity factors from NREL LCA Harmonisation and IEA technology reviews, representing 2023–24 global average compositions.

kg_m = Σ (intensity_m,t × capacity_t)
e.g. Silicon = 5.5 kg/kWp × 40 kWp = 220 kg
e.g. Cobalt = 0.068 kg/kWh × 100 kWh = 6.8 kg

Intensity factors carry ±15–25% uncertainty. Values: IEA/NREL confidence level 3–5.

Stage 2 + 3
Dimension scoring + aggregation

Each material scored on 6 dimensions (0–100, higher = more risk). Mass-weighted average across materials gives the building-level dimension score. Dimension scores combined with policy-derived weights give CMES.
